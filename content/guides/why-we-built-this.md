I kept losing bridge hands because I couldn't remember my own conventions.

Not in some abstract way. I mean I'd sit down, pick up a nice hand, partner opens 1NT, and I'd blank on whether we play Smolen. I learned it once. I read the BridgeWinners article, I talked it through with partner over lunch, I thought I had it. But at the table, with thirteen cards in my hand and three people waiting, I wasn't sure. So I just bid 3H and hoped for the best.

That happened enough times that I started looking for a way to practice. Like, actually practice. Not "play fifty hands on BBO and hope the right auction comes up" practice, but "show me twenty Smolen hands in a row until I stop freezing" practice.

It doesn't exist. I looked everywhere.

BBO is great for playing, but it's not a practice tool. Funbridge has lessons and post-hand analysis, but you can't isolate a single convention and drill it. Cuebids is excellent for partnership bidding, but you need an actual partner online at the same time, and it's still full auctions, not targeted reps. No Fear Bridge, BridgeBee, the books, the YouTube channels. Tons of great educational material out there. But education and practice are different things. You don't learn guitar by reading about chord shapes. At some point you have to put your hands on the strings and do it a hundred times.

The other thing that bugged me was that every tool out there assumes you play one system. But I play SAYC at the Tuesday club game and 2/1 with my regular partner on weekends. We play Jacoby Transfers but not Smolen (well, now we do, see above). Our 1NT range is 15-17, not 16-18. Every partnership has little tweaks, and if you're practicing against a system that doesn't match your actual card, you might be building the wrong muscle memory. That's arguably worse than not practicing at all.

So I built something.

## What it does

You pick a convention. The app generates hands that actually test that convention's decision points. You bid. It tells you whether you got it right, and more importantly, it tells you why. Not just "wrong, try again" but the full reasoning: here's what the convention was looking for, here's what your hand had, here's why 2D was the right call and 2H wasn't.

The library covers the common stuff, obviously, but the whole point is that it also covers the conventions nobody else bothers with. The ones that are hard to find practice material for anywhere. The ones you added to your card six months ago and have seen come up maybe twice. Those are the ones where reps matter most, and those are the ones I keep adding.

You can practice as opener or responder. There's a mode where the auction is already set up and you just find the right bid, and a mode where you bid the whole sequence from the top. Each convention has a learning page that walks through the logic as a flow tree before you start drilling, so you're not going in cold.

And because every partnership is a little different, you can build your own system. Start from a base, swap in the modules you actually play, adjust your ranges. Practice against your card, not someone else's.

## Where it's going

The learning pages are free, and they'll stay free. Bridge conventions are public knowledge, and the explanations should be too. The practice side is where the product lives. That feedback loop, the hand generation, the bid evaluation, the ability to configure your system and drill specific weak spots, that's the thing I haven't found anywhere else, and that's the thing worth building well.

I'm not trying to be the place where you learn bridge from scratch. There are better resources for that. I'm trying to be the place you come back to three times a week to stay sharp on the conventions you already play. The gap between "I've read about this" and "I can do this at the table without thinking" is just reps, and this is a reps machine.

It's free to learn on, you don't need a partner, and you can start right now.
