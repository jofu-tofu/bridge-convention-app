# Deep Expansion — Agent D (backward citation chaining)

Traces ancestor work cited by the seed papers. Focus: choice-overload
originals, default-effect foundations, progressive-disclosure roots,
deliberate-practice canon, and SRL theoretical bases.

## Papers Found

### 1. Iyengar, S. S., & Lepper, M. R. (2000) "When Choice Is Demotivating: Can One Desire Too Much of a Good Thing?"
- **Found Via:** Cited by Jameson (2014) monograph, Schwartz Paradox of Choice, and the JMIR Digital Choice Architecture seed (#15). Foundational empirical anchor for choice-overload arguments used in seeds #1, #7, #15.
- **Key Finding:** The "jam study" — shoppers shown 24 jams browsed more but purchased far less (3%) than those shown 6 jams (40%); participants with smaller option sets also reported higher satisfaction and wrote better essays when given fewer optional topics. Three experiments, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 79(6):995–1006.
- **Source:** https://faculty.washington.edu/jdb/345/345%20Articles/Iyengar%20&%20Lepper%20(2000).pdf

### 2. Scheibehenne, B., Greifeneder, R., & Todd, P. M. (2010) "Can There Ever Be Too Many Options? A Meta-Analytic Review of Choice Overload"
- **Found Via:** Standard counter-citation in any Iyengar/Schwartz discussion; cited by Jameson (2014) and the Jesse & Jannach (2021) digital-nudging survey (seed #12).
- **Key Finding:** Meta-analysis of 63 conditions from 50 experiments (N=5,036). Mean effect size of choice overload ≈ zero with high between-study variance. Overload appears only under specific preconditions (time pressure, no prior preference, difficulty of comparison) — not a general law. Directly tempers any "always shrink the menu" prescription.
- **Source:** https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/37/3/409/1827647 (PDF: https://scheibehenne.com/ScheibehenneGreifenederTodd2010.pdf)

### 3. Schwartz, B. (2004) *The Paradox of Choice: Why More Is Less*
- **Found Via:** Popular-press ancestor cited by Jameson (2014) ASPECT/ARCADE framework and by nearly every digital-nudging paper including Jesse & Jannach (2021) (seed #12).
- **Key Finding:** Distinguishes **maximizers** (seek optimal) from **satisficers** (seek good-enough); argues large option sets disproportionately harm maximizers via regret and opportunity cost. Provides the vocabulary ("satisficing", "maximizer") that later choice-architecture work uses when recommending defaults.
- **Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Paradox_of_Choice (book, Harper Perennial)

### 4. Johnson, E. J., & Goldstein, D. (2003) "Do Defaults Save Lives?"
- **Found Via:** Foundational default-effect citation in Jameson (2014), Thaler & Sunstein *Nudge*, Jesse & Jannach (2021) seed #12, and the JMIR Digital Choice Architecture piece seed #15.
- **Key Finding:** Organ-donation rates in opt-out countries (~90–99%) vs opt-in countries (~4–28%); holds across three studies including a hypothetical-vignette experiment controlling for cultural confounds. The canonical evidence that default selections drive behavior without restricting freedom.
- **Source:** https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1091721

### 5. Thaler, R. H., & Sunstein, C. R. (2008) *Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness*
- **Found Via:** Source for the term "choice architecture" that titles seed #7 (Jameson). Also cited by seeds #12 (Jesse & Jannach nudging survey) and #15 (JMIR medical-education choice architecture).
- **Key Finding:** Introduces libertarian paternalism: choice architects can structure defaults, framing, and salience to improve outcomes without removing options. Taxonomy of nudge mechanisms (defaults, anchoring, feedback, expect-error, give-structure-to-complex-choices, incentives) that Jesse & Jannach later operationalize for recommender UIs.
- **Source:** Yale University Press, 2008. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nudge_(book)

### 6. Ericsson, K. A., Krampe, R. Th., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993) "The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance"
- **Found Via:** Foundational citation underneath Goldberg et al. (2023) "Drill-Practice-Repeat" (seed #14) and Bjork & Bjork desirable-difficulties chapter (seed #11). Every deliberate-practice argument in the drill-hub literature ultimately cites this paper.
- **Key Finding:** Introduces deliberate practice: effortful, goal-directed, feedback-rich activity *designed* to improve performance — distinct from mere play or routine work. Violin-student study showed strong dose-response between accumulated deliberate-practice hours and attained skill tier. Provides the theoretical warrant for "competency-selection surface" framing used by Goldberg.
- **Source:** *Psychological Review* 100(3):363–406. https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1993-40718-001

### 7. Bjork, R. A. (1994) "Memory and Metamemory Considerations in the Training of Human Beings"
- **Found Via:** Original source of "desirable difficulties" — the concept seed #11 builds on. Kornell & Bjork (2008) and later Bjork & Bjork (2011) chapters explicitly cite this as origin.
- **Key Finding:** Coins "desirable difficulties" — manipulations that impede performance during training (spacing, interleaving, variation, generation, reduced feedback) facilitate long-term retention and transfer. Learner introspection systematically favors easier conditions that produce worse outcomes. Underpins the "interleaved default" argument in seed #11.
- **Source:** In J. Metcalfe & A. Shimamura (Eds.), *Metacognition: Knowing About Knowing* (pp. 185–205), MIT Press. https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/1994-bjork.pdf

### 8. Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008) "Learning Concepts and Categories: Is Spacing the 'Enemy of Induction'?"
- **Found Via:** Primary empirical study underneath the Bjork & Bjork (2011) + Kirk-Johnson seed #11 on learners-recognize-but-don't-use effective practice.
- **Key Finding:** Interleaved exposure to painters' works produced better later classification of novel paintings than blocked exposure — yet participants rated blocked study as more effective *after* their own test performance contradicted that belief. The canonical metacognitive-illusion result behind "learners self-select poorly."
- **Source:** *Psychological Science* 19(6):585–592. https://web.williams.edu/Psychology/Faculty/Kornell/Publications/Kornell.Bjork.2008a.pdf

### 9. Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013) "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques"
- **Found Via:** Cited by seed #11 (Bjork/Kirk-Johnson) and by Jivet et al. (2023) seed #10 as the reference taxonomy for which study behaviors merit dashboard support.
- **Key Finding:** Rates 10 study techniques on utility. **High utility:** practice testing, distributed practice. **Moderate:** elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, interleaved practice. **Low:** summarization, highlighting, rereading, imagery, keyword mnemonics. Establishes the canonical "what effective practice actually looks like" list that any recommendation layer should nudge toward.
- **Source:** *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* 14(1):4–58. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1529100612453266

### 10. Zimmerman, B. J. (2000/2002) Cyclical Phases Model of Self-Regulated Learning
- **Found Via:** Primary theoretical scaffold underneath Kizilcec et al. SRL review (seed #9) and Jivet et al. LAD review (seed #10). Both reviews classify dashboard interventions against Zimmerman's three phases.
- **Key Finding:** Three-phase cycle — **forethought** (goal setting, strategic planning, self-efficacy), **performance** (self-observation, self-control), **self-reflection** (self-judgment, self-reaction) — operating recursively. Defines the placement slots where recommendations, progress signals, and review prompts enter the learning loop; explains why a badge without an action fails (it's a signal in the self-reflection phase that doesn't connect to forethought).
- **Source:** Zimmerman, B. J. (2002). "Becoming a self-regulated learner: An overview." *Theory into Practice* 41(2):64–70. Legacy review: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-025-10052-0

### 11. Winne, P. H., & Hadwin, A. F. (1998) COPES Model of Self-Regulated Learning
- **Found Via:** Second major SRL framework cited alongside Zimmerman in Kizilcec et al. (seed #9) and Jivet et al. (seed #10); the latter explicitly uses Winne's four-phase scheme to classify learning-analytics interventions.
- **Key Finding:** Studying as information processing with **C**onditions, **O**perations, **P**roducts, **E**valuations, **S**tandards across four weakly-ordered phases (task definition, goal setting/planning, enactment, adaptation). "Conditions" decomposes into cognitive (beliefs, prior knowledge, tactic knowledge) and task (resources, instructional cues, time) — giving design vocabulary for *which* condition a picker-screen nudge targets.
- **Source:** In D. Schunk & B. Zimmerman (Eds.), *Self-Regulated Learning: From Teaching to Self-Reflective Practice*. Review: https://ssrlsig.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/greene-azevedo-2007-a-theor-review-of-winne-and-hadwins-model-of-srl.pdf

### 12. Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000) Self-Determination Theory — Autonomy, Competence, Relatedness
- **Found Via:** Cited by Kizilcec et al. SRL review (seed #9) as the motivational-theory basis for the "user-initiated support preserves autonomy" finding. Also underneath Duolingo-style learner-control arguments in seeds #1 and #20.
- **Key Finding:** Three basic psychological needs — **autonomy** (self-endorsed action), **competence** (mastery feedback), **relatedness** (belonging) — are necessary for intrinsic motivation. Support for autonomy predicts persistence; controlling environments (proactive prompts, forced paths) undermine it. Directly explains the Duolingo tree-to-path tradeoff: guidance helped proficiency but cost autonomy-motivated users.
- **Source:** *Contemporary Educational Psychology* 25:54–67 (2000); *American Psychologist* 55:68–78. https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2000_RyanDeci_SDT.pdf

### 13. Sweller, J. (1988 / 1998 with van Merriënboer & Paas) Cognitive Load Theory
- **Found Via:** Load-bearing citation in Alhadreti & Elbahi (2023) mobile-learning UI SLR (seed #17), Zhang/Yu/Chen (2021) gamified-microlearning SLR (seed #18), and the JMIR digital-choice-architecture piece (seed #15) on "minimize unnecessary choices that deplete cognitive resources."
- **Key Finding:** Working memory is sharply limited; instructional design must minimize **extraneous load** (irrelevant processing caused by presentation), calibrate **intrinsic load** (element interactivity of the material) to learner expertise, and free capacity for **germane load** (schema construction). Justifies sectioning, density limits, and progressive disclosure on practice-picker screens.
- **Source:** Sweller (1988) *Cognitive Science* 12:257–285; Sweller, van Merriënboer, Paas (1998) *Educational Psychology Review* 10:251–296. Overview: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9128-5

### 14. Nielsen, J. (1995/2006) "Progressive Disclosure"
- **Found Via:** Cited by seed #3 (NN/g cards vs list) and seed #4 (Pernice 8 guidelines for complex apps). The NN/g guideline corpus underneath Practice-tab design decisions.
- **Key Finding:** Defer advanced or rarely-used features to secondary screens to reduce error rates and speed novice learning. **Hard limit:** two disclosure levels; three or more levels cause users to get lost — if unavoidable, chunk by card-sort-validated groupings. Directly argues for flat category groupings on the drill picker rather than nested drill-down.
- **Source:** https://www.nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure/

### 15. Scheiter, K., & Gerjets, P. (2007) "Learner Control in Hypermedia Environments"
- **Found Via:** Cited by Alhadreti & Elbahi (seed #17) and the broader hypermedia/learner-control literature underneath the Duolingo guided-path debate (seed #1).
- **Key Finding:** Comprehensive review — high learner control in hypermedia *seldom* produces its theorized motivation/adaptation benefits. Learners with low prior knowledge or weak metacognitive skills navigate poorly, skipping prerequisites and underusing adaptive features. Provides the empirical warrant for system-curated sequencing (single-path playlist, guided path) over pure free-browse for novices.
- **Source:** *Educational Psychology Review* 19:285–307. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-007-9046-3

  - Cognitive-load / progressive-disclosure / learner-control roots: Sweller CLT (13), Nielsen progressive disclosure (14), Scheiter & Gerjets (15).
- **Notable observation:** The Scheibehenne et al. (2010) meta-analysis is the single most important *counterweight* uncovered — it reduces the Iyengar jam-study effect to a conditional one, which tightens (rather than weakens) the design argument: choice overload is real on the Practice tab *only* when users lack prior preference and face time/comparison pressure. This matches the intermediate-player persona exactly.
- **Path notes (2-hop):** Ryan & Deci SDT → Kizilcec SRL review (seed #9); Sweller CLT → Alhadreti mobile-UI SLR (seed #17); Ericsson 1993 → Goldberg 2023 drill-practice framework (seed #14); Bjork 1994 → Kornell & Bjork 2008 → Bjork & Bjork 2011 chapter (seed #11).
- **"Hub-and-spoke" backward search came up empty** in peer-reviewed form — it is primarily a practitioner/NN-g term (Pernice, seed #4) without a distinct academic ancestor beyond general progressive-disclosure and information-architecture literature. Worth flagging to Wave 3 as a terminology-only concept.
